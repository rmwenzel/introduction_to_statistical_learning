What is Statistical Learning?

Given paired data (X,Y)(X, Y)(X,Y), assume a relationship between XXX1 and YYY modeled by

Y=f(X)+ϵ Y = f(X) + \epsilon Y=f(X)+ϵ

where f:Rp→Rf:\mathbb{R}^p \rightarrow \mathbb{R}f:Rp→R is a function and ϵ\epsilonϵ is a random error term with E(ϵ)=0\mathbb{E}(\epsilon) = 0E(ϵ)=0.

Statistical learning is a set of approaches for estimating fff2

Why Estimate f?

Prediction

  • We may want to predict the output YYY from an estimate f^\hat{f}f^​ of fff. The predicted value for a given YYY is then Y^=f^(X) \hat{Y} = \hat{f}(X)Y^=f^​(X). In prediction, we often treat fff as a black-box

  • The mean squared-error3 mse(Y^)=E(Y−Y^)2\mathbf{mse}(\hat{Y})=\mathbb{E}(Y-\hat{Y})^2mse(Y^)=E(Y−Y^)2 is a good measure of the accuracy of Y^\hat{Y}Y^ as a predictor for YYY.

  • One can write

mse(Y^)=(f(X)−f^(X))2+V(ϵ) \mathbf{mse}(\hat{Y}) = (f(X) - \hat{f}(X))^2 + \mathbb{V}(\epsilon) mse(Y^)=(f(X)−f^​(X))2+V(ϵ)

These two terms are known as the reducible error and irreducible error, respectively4

Inference
  • Instead of predicting YYY from XXX, we may be more interested how YYY changes as a function of XXX. In inference, we usually do not treat fff as a black box.

Examples of important inference questions:

  • Which predictors have the largest influence on the response?
  • What is the relationship between the response and each predictor?
  • Is f linear or non-linear?

How to Estimate f?

Parametric methods

Steps for parametric method:

  1. Assume a parametric model for fff, that is assume a specific functional form5

f=f(X,β)f = f(X, \boldsymbol{\beta}) f=f(X,β)

for some vector of parameters β=(β1,…,βp)T\boldsymbol{\beta} = (\beta_1,\dots,\beta_p)^Tβ=(β1​,…,βp​)T

  1. Use the training data to fit or train the model, that is to choose βi\beta_iβi​ such that

Y≈f(X,β)Y \approx f(X, \boldsymbol{\beta})Y≈f(X,β)

Non-parametric methods

These methods make no assumptions about the functional form of fff.

Accuracy vs. Interpretability

  • In inference, generally speaking the more flexible the method, the less interpretable.

  • In prediction, generally speaking the more flexible the method, the less accurate

Supervised vs. Unsupervised Learning

  • In supervised learning, training data consists of pairs (X,Y)(X, Y)(X,Y) where XXX is a vector of predictors and YYY a response. Prediction and inference are supervised learning problems, and the response variable (or the relationship between the response and the predictors) supervises the analysis of model

  • In unsupervised learning, training data lacks a response variable.

Regression vs. Classification

  • Problems with a quantitative response (Y∈S⊆RY\in S \subseteq \mathbb{R}Y∈S⊆R) tend to be called regression problems

  • Problems with a qualitative, or categorical response (Y∈{y1,…,yn})Y \in \{y_1, \dots, y_n\})Y∈{y1​,…,yn​}) tend to be called classification problems

Assessing Model Accuracy

There is no free lunch in statistics

  • In regression, the most common measure is the mean-squared error

  • We are usually not interested in minimizing MSE with respect to training data but rather to test data.

  • There is no guarantee low training MSE will translate to low test MSE.

  • Having low training MSE but high test MSE is called overfitting
